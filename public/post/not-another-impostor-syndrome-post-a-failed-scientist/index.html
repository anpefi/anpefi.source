Not another Impostor Syndrome post: a failed scientist

2018-01-02 2 min read career

How long you can be a postdoc? 5, 10, 15 years?

I wouldn’t say “I’m a fraud”. I don’t believe that. I know well my strengths and my weaknesses. This is not a post about I’m suffering the called impostor syndrome. I’m not. I really failed to become a scientist. More properly, I failed to build an successful academic career in science. I would like to explain the how and why. This is jut for me, to expose my feelings about the current point in my career. However, if this could help you to analyze your goals and, maybe, avoid some of the mistakes I think I made, then I would be greatly satisfied.

tl;dr

I earned my PhD 12 years ago, in 2005. I am currently working as a postdoc for a project where I am not PI. This is my 7th or 8th postdoc (I’ve really lost the account), and like most of them, linked to my alma mater. My contract expires at the end of 2018. I never earned a research grant (just a couple of fellowships). I cannot apply for most of the fellowships to start an independent PI career as I’m too old (>10 years) and my CV has several deficiencies. And I have virtually no chance of getting a permanent position in the academy. Therefore, I am in a dead end. But I am the only one responsible for this situation. Some bad decisions, together with the bad luck of the conjuncture (the great economic crisis since 2008) and some personal limitations (my partner got a fixed job fairly soon, in 2007) are the causes why I was not able to develop a consistent scientific / academic career. So far I have been surviving from project to project thanks to a series of technical skills. However, these continuous changes of lines of research (none of my own) have also been weighing my already battered possibilities of becoming an IP. Without remorse, I will continue working on different projects while someone continues to have an interest in my limited abilities. However, already with 40 years and a family, I look for alternatives away from academia and science. Of course, I have enjoyed doing what I really like all these years.
